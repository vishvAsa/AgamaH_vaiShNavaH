These excerpts are from Joseph T. O’Connell arguing against labeling Rāmānanda as Sahajiyā and framing his practices within rāgānuga-bhakti instead.

The context for Rāmānanda’s peculiar sādhana thus becomes clearer, given the conventions of the temple complex at Puri. There was a tradition of temple dancers-cum-singers, called devadāsīs.¹³ They would perform in turns the ritual of dance and song in the Jagannātha temple. And they would perform other pieces outside the temple upon request.

While a devadāsī might well instruct her own daughter or protégée in the standard ritualized instruction, it evidently was possible for a male instructor to offer additional instruction, especially for sophisticated compositions beyond the standard ritual repertoire. Rāmānanda Rāya as author of a celebrated devotional drama, Jagannātha-vallabha-nāṭaka, with its many Sanskrit songs seems to have been eager that his own compositions be performed as flawlessly as possible. As a devotee he was concerned that the song, dance and dramatization should reflect faithfully the proper devotional nuances of the Krishna līlās in accordance with the refinements of bhakti-rasa-śāstra (teachings on devotional sentiments).

A male instructor of a female dancer in India or elsewhere may be expected in the course of his work to touch, observe closely and establish a close teacher-student relationship with his pupil.

In such a professional relationship between an adult man and a younger girl there lies the possibility of abuse and exploitation, but one assumes that in the main the professional figure (be he instructor, doctor or whatever) should maintain control over his feelings and actions in the interests of his client/patient/student.

Rāmānanda is reported to have maintained such control to an extreme degree, the very extremity of which raises the question of possible Sahajiyā involvement.

Bathing the girls, massaging them and touching their private parts even though with no more sexual interest than if he (or they) were a log, does seem to go beyond the normal duties of the male instructor in the dance.

A Sahajiyā or a Rāgānuga Bhakta?

(Page 49)

However, it is not unprecedented in India for a man, be he a formal ascetic or even a man of some public responsibilities, to consider sexual continence (or feeling and imagination as well as of overt physical action) to be a major constituent of his personal discipline and to test himself in appropriate ways to reassure and further steel himself in this regard. While left Tantrics may make a ritualized routine of such testing, they have no monopoly on the basic idea and practice of testing one’s sexual control.

Rāmānanda’s behaviour, peculiar as it may at first seem, bears no resemblance to the ritualized Tantric mode of worship of the girl/goddess by the male adept. It is an extension of the physical training and aesthetic instruction of dancing girls by their mentor. To invite devadāsīs to one’s home or pilgrim hostel to perform devotional song and/or dance was an established and respected custom in Puri, and a significant source of income for devadāsīs.

Furthermore, it was customary for pilgrims to worship them (as in some sense the brides of Lord Jagannātha), to do pūjā to them, to take (or roll in) the dust of their feet. In other parts of India (e.g., Mathurā and Vṛndāvana) it was and is customary to offer pūjā to the boy actors when they are in the roles Krishna and Rādhā or Rāma and Sītā. Such worship of devadāsīs and rās līlā performers is standard popular Hindu piety.

An essential characteristic of Rāmānanda’s way of testing himself and of worshipping the devadāsīs was that he acted toward the girls as their female servant, a dāsī. This is diametrically opposed to the relationship of the Sahajiyā adept, who accentuates his (divinized) maleness in relation to the (divinized) girl who is his partner in ritual.

It is unlikely that the kind of servant woman he had in mind was merely the human woman of Puri ministering to her devadāsī protégée’s needs. It was much more likely the transcendent cowherd woman of the eternal Vraja, the lesser gopī, who as sakhī (friend), mañjarī (maiden), or simply dāsī (servant girl) waited upon the more prominent divine gopīs, even the ultimate gopī, Rādhā.

That Rāmānanda had some such intentionality in mind during his sādhana of service to the devadāsīs—girls whom he had trained to depict the actions and feelings of the gopīs, presumably featuring Rādhā—is quite likely in view of his celebrated discourse with Caitanya and other indicators of his piety.

In that discourse (which I presume reflects the general tenor of Rāmānanda’s ideas, piety and relationship to Caitanya, however much details may have been altered in Kṛṣṇadāsa’s recasting a century later) Rāmānanda is presented as giving major importance to the female friends, i.e., the secondary gopīs (cowherdesses) who support in myriad ways Rādhā and Krishna in their eternal sports of divine love. (Page 50)

Furthermore Rāmānanda is shown recommending rāgānuga-sādhana-bhakti, i.e., the devotional discipline of following the intense devotion of such eternal devotees as the sakhīs. Their eternal devotion, by contrast, is called rāgātmikā, devotion that is innate—not acquired by imitation.¹⁴ Rāmānanda’s own sādhana, peculiar as it at first appears, becomes quite intelligible as an idiosyncratic exemplification of precisely this rāgānuga-sādhana-bhakti.¹⁵

There soon developed within the Caitanyaite Vaiṣṇava community a special type of devotional meditation, rāgānuga-sādhana-bhakti, whereby the human devotee systematically would strive to imitate/follow (in his meditations) the devotional feelings believed to be perpetually expressed by one or another of the loving associates of Krishna in various divine sports, especially those associated with the eternal Vraja and characterized by mādhurya, delicacy. The closer we get to them, the more the features of Rāmānanda’s sādhana point away from, rather than toward, a Sahajiyā orientation. There is no indication that Rāmānanda conceived of himself as male in relation to the girls in the sādhana.
