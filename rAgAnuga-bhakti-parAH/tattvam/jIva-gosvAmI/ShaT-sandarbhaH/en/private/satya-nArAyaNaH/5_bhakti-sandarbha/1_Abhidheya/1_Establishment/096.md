The sense of the verse is as follows: "O Unlimited Lord (bhuman), previously there were indeed many yogis in this world. Having failed to attain realized knowledge (jñāna) through their practice of yoga, they later offered their prescribed practices and even their worldly actions unto You [the word ihā, 'effort or exertion,' implies both types of action]. As a consequence of thus surrendering their actions unto You, they obtained bhakti in the form of the taste (ruci) for hearing Your narrations (katha). Furthermore, on account of the contact (samipam, lit., proximity') with such katha, they were led to attain bhakti in the form of a liking (ruci) for You directly, the subject of such narrations (kathaniya).? By this bhakti alone [consisting of these two types of taste], they easily realized the Absolute, beginning from atma-tattva, 'the immediate intuition of the self,' up to bhagavat-tattva, 'the immediate intuition of Bhagavän.' Having done so, they attained Your supreme intrinsic [i.e., supramundane] destination (paramantarangāṁ gatim)."

Commentary 
IN THE previous anuccheda, Śrī Jīva Gosvāmi made the point that the practices that constitute the path of dharma, or "sanctified action," bear real fruit only if performed for the satisfaction of Bhagavan. Now he goes a step further to assert that any attempt to cultivate jñāna, or "immediate awareness of the self," without bhakti will also end in failure. In the main verse of this anuccheda (SB 10.14.5), Brahmā himself admits that in the past many yogis were unsuccessful in their attempts to attain such realization through their practice of yoga. Being thus frustrated, they later offered all of their actions and aspirations to Bhagavan. 
This attestation serves as a lesson for modern-day practitioners. By offering one's actions to Bhagavan, and by the association of a devotee, a person will obtain faith in the process of hearing about Kṛṣṇa. This will lead to constancy in hearing from realized devotees and engagement in sadhana-bhakti. This in turn will purify the heart of all unwanted predispositions and desires (vāsanās). Then, by the grace of bhakti, the practitioner will be blessed with direct experience of Bhagavan. Thus, true jñāna ensues only from bhakti. This is confirmed by Krsna in the verse cited here (GĪTĀ 10.11). Any practice devoid of bhakti is termed as ignorance (ajñānam) by Kṛṣṇa in the following pair of verses:
