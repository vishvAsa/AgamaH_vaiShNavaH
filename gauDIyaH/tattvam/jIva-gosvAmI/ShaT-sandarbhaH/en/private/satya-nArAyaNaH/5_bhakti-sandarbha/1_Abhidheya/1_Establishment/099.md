Bhakti Contrasted with Karma 
९९ । अथ व्यतिरेकेण कर्मानादरेणाह - तत्र कर्मणः फलप्राप्तावनिश्चयवत्त्वं दुःखरूपत्वं च भक्तेस्तु तस्यामावश्यकत्वं साधकदशायामपि सुखरूपत्वं चेत्याह ( भा० १११८ ११२ ) - 
NOW BHAKTI is established as the abhidheya through negative concomitance (vyatireka), by expressing disregard for the path of karma. There is both uncertainty and misery involved in attaining the results of karma. But in the case of bhakti, suc- cess is assured and the devotee experiences joy even in the stage of practice. These conclusions are summed up in the following statement of the sage Saunaka to Sūta Gosvāmi: 
कर्मण्यस्मिन्ननाश्वासे धूमधूम्रात्मनां भवान् । आपाययति गोविन्दपादपद्मासवं मधु || १७९ ।। 
We have been engaged in the performance of this sacrifice, the fruits of which are uncertain, until our bodies were blackened by smoke. Now you have come and revived us by causing us to drink the ambrosial nectar flowing from Śrī Govinda's lotus feet. (SB 1.18.12)1 
अस्मिन् कर्मणि सत्रेऽनाश्वासेऽविश्वसनीये वैगुण्यबाहुल्येन कृषिवत् फलनिश्चयाभा- वात् । अनेन भक्तेर्विश्वसनीयत्वं ध्वनितम् । धूमेन धूम्रो विरञ्जितौ विवर्णावात्मानौ शरीरचित्ते येषाम् । कर्मणि षष्ठी तानस्मानित्यर्थः । पादपद्मस्य यशोरूपमासर्व मकरन्द मधु मधुरम् । अत्र सत्रवत् कर्मान्तरं यशःश्रवणवद् भक्त्यन्तरं चेति ज्ञेयम् । तदेवं भक्तिविनाभूतानां कर्मादिभिरस्माकं दुःखमेवासीदिति व्यतिरेकत्वमत्र गम्यते ।

In this endeavor (asmin karmani), meaning "in this sacrifice" (asmin satre), the outcome is "unassured" (anāśvāse), or "unre- liable" (aviśvasaniya). Because of the possibility of many dis- crepancies (vaigunya-bahulyena) in the performance of sacri- fice, there is a want of certainty in regard to the result (phala- niścayābhāvāt), as is the case with farming. By this statement, the reliability (viśvasanīyatvam) of bhakti is indirectly implied. 
In the compound dhuma-dhumratmanām, the word ātmanām refers to "the body and mind" (sarira-citte), dhumra means "dispirited" (virañjita) and "discolored" (vivarna), and dhuma means "by the smoke" (dhūmena). Thus, the compound as a whole means "to us, who have become dispirited and discol- ored in mind and body due to the smoke." Although ātmanām appears in the sixth case [the possessive case], it is used in the sense of the accusative, [indicating that the sages are the direct object of the verb āpāyayati, “You are causing us to drink"]. In the compound pada-padmāsavam, the word asavam means, "the intoxicating nectar of a flower" (makarandam), which flows from the lotus feet (pada-padma) of Śrī Govinda in the form of His fame (yaso-rupam). This nectar, or fame, is "honey-like" or "ambrosial" (madhu). 
In this statement, it is to be understood that the uncertainty and hardship that is specifically pointed out in regard to the satra sacrifice actually applies to all other forms of karma. Similarly, the certainty and pleasure attributed to hearing the glories of Bhagavan applies to all other types of bhakti. Thus, by negative concomitance (vyatireka), it is understood from Saunaka's state- ment that by the execution of karma alone devoid of bhakti, the performers experienced only misery.

Thousands of sages headed by Saunaka had assembled at Nai- miṣāranya to participate in a long-enduring yajña, specified by the name satra. According to Vyasa's statement in the beginning of Srimad Bhāgavata, it was meant to last for a thousand years (SB 1.1.4). The sages, however, were unsure that they would obtain the desired result. On this account, they referred to the sacrifice by the word anāśvāse, or "uncertain." They also found the whole endeavor to be quite troublesome, and thus they admitted that their minds and bodies had become covered in soot from the smoke of the sacrificial fire. It is understandable that their bodies became covered in soot, but how could such a thing be said of their minds? This indicates that they were feeling mental dissatisfaction and uncertainty in regard to the result. In this connection, Śrī Jīva glosses the word dhümra, lit., "smoke-colored," with the two words. viranjita and vivarna. Although both of these words mean "dis- colored," the first word in particular can also carry the sense of "dispirited" or "disheartened," which is perhaps what Śrī Jiva had in mind in supplying two otherwise perfectly synonymous terms. 
The path of sacrificial action (karma-marga) is so precise that if the ritual is not executed exactly as prescribed down to the last detail, it will not yield the desired result. A slight deviation even in the intonation of a single phoneme in a mantra may even bring to bear the exact opposite of what was intended. The story of the demon Vṛtrāsura, who was generated from a dark tantrika ritual, and that of Diti, who contrived to conceive a son who would be able to kill Indra, are glaring examples of the fallibility of karma. In con- trast, bhakti is pleasurable even during the stage of practice, and its result is assured because of the supreme independent power with which it is intrinsically endowed. If there is any imperfection in the course of the performance of bhakti, Bhagavan personally brings about its completion, as confirmed by the sage Karabhājana:
