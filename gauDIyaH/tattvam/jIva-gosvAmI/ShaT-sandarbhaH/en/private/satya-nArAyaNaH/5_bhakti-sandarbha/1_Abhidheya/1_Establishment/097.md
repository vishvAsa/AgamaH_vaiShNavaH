Bhakti Is the Source of All Perfection 
९७ । यान्यन्यानि सर्वाणि तत्र पुरुषार्थसाधनान्युच्यन्ते तान्यपि तथैव भक्तिमूलान्येव । यथा ( भा० १० । ८१ ।१९ ) - 
ALL other practices (sādhanas) that are described in Śrīmad Bhāgavata as productive of the ultimate good (puruṣārtha) are similarly rooted only in bhakti, as expressed in these words of Śrīdāmā Vipra: 
स्वर्गापवर्गयोः पुंसां रसायां भुवि सम्पदाम् । 
सर्वासामपि सिद्धीनां मूलं तच्चरणार्चनम् || १७१ || 
Worship of Your lotus feet is the root of all perfections and opulences that human beings can attain in heaven, in liberation, on earth, or in the subterrestrial realms. (SB 10.81.19 ) 1

Additionally, bhakti's perpetuity (nityatva), meaning, its regular compulsory nature, is expressed in this pair of verses: 
From the mouth, arms, thighs, and feet of the Purusa appeared the four respective vocational classifications (varnas) along with the four stages of life (asramas). The varnas were distinctively divided into vipra, ksatriya, vaisya, and sūdra according to the corresponding gunas that determine their natures. Among human beings belonging to these divisions, those who [naively] neglect to worship the Purușa, who is directly the primordial ground of being of all individual beings (atmaprabhavam) and the Supreme Immanent Regulator of all being (Iśvara), or who [knowingly] show Him contempt, are deprived of their status and fall into degradation. (SB 11.5.2-3)3

Bhagavan is the Supreme Master and Regulator of everything. We have the capacity to act, but the result is not in our control. This is clearly expressed by Sri Krsna in the Gita: "Your eligibility pertains exclusively to the fulfillment of action but never to the fruits thereof" (GITA 2.47)? There are many factors involved in the outcome of actions, such as time and place. Beyond all other material considerations, there is the overseership of the Supreme Immanent Self, Paramātmā. Everything functions under His supervision. Therefore, no result can come about without His consent. Sudama thus concludes that all perfections are rooted in the worship of Kṛṣṇa's feet (SB 10.81.19). 
Śrī Jiva then cites three verses to show that bhakti is a compulsory duty, without which all other prescribed duties are flawed. The first verse (SB 8.23.16) is spoken by Sukrācārya, the guru of Bali, who was the king of the asuras. Bali's yajña was interrupted by Vamanadeva when He went there to beg land from Bali. Bhagavan Vamanadeva had requested Sukracārya to complete any deficiency that might occur in the yajña. In response to this proposal, Šukrācārya spoke this verse. Sukrācārya declared that all perfection comes about merely by chanting the name of Bhagavan. Those seeking material rewards alone can obtain the results of their respective undertakings by the grace of bhakti, but if one performs the same actions as an offering to Bhagavan, one will also attain bhakti in course of time. 
The next pair of verses cited by Śrī Jiva (SB 11.5.2-3) is spoken by the sage Camasa. He makes it emphatically clear that without devotion to the Supreme Self, who is the very source of all being, nobody can succeed in any endeavor. Indeed, even if one has attained a high position, he will lose it. This is further confirmed by King Yudhisthira (SB 10.72.4). In this manner, because bhakti is shown
