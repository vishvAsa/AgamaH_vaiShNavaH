I Bhakti Is the Complete Abhidheya 
As bhakti is so much emphasized, even those who are not overly discerning worship Bhagavan Sri Visņu, yet they do so only as a part of karma, or as part of whatever path they are following, in order to achieve the results of their respective practice. This, however, is an offense to bhakti. As a result, bhakti awards them only with the fruit of their respective desires, and of that also there is no assurance. But, if bhakti is performed independent of those practices, even if only to attain the results proclaimed by those paths, bhakti certainly awards those results. Moreover, bhakti is not limited merely to offering those particular fruits, rather in the end it bestows the supreme fruit as well.

Being prayed to by human beings, Bhagavan truly indeed (satyam eva) awards the results that they seek. The word satyam, "it is true," indicates that this cannot ever be otherwise. Yet, by doing so, He does not offer anything of genuine value, because even after awarding such boons, He is not relieved from their solicitations. The reason for this is that the worshiper remains unfulfilled by obtaining his desired object, and when his enjoyment is exhausted, he again prays to Bhagavan for some new object. This pattern is illustrated in the following statement: "The craving for sensual gratification is never extinguished by enjoyment of the sense objects" (SB 9.19.14). 
Recognizing this pattern, the most merciful Bhagavan awards His own lotus feet, which are here described as iccha-pidhānam, that which brings an end to all desires. This means that He offers His lotus feet to the worshiper even though the person doesn't desire them due to ignorance of their sweetness. This is comparable to a mother's action of removing a chewed piece of clay from the mouth of her child and offering a sweet in its place.

Accordingly, it is to be understood that even Śrī Sanaka and the other Kumāra brothers, who were established in the immediate realization of Brahman (brahma-jñānīs), attained Bhagavan's lotus feet by their adherence to bhakti [even though they originally did so only to attain Brahman realization]. 
Commentary 
IN THIS ANUCCHEDA, Śrī Jiva clearly demonstrates that bhakti is supremely independent in awarding its results, whereas all other processes are impotent without bhakti. From this, it is understood that one should engage only in bhakti, no matter what one's aspired for end might be. This is the import of the akāma verse (SB 2.3.10) spoken by Sukadeva and already discussed earlier (in Anuccheda 31). 
There are three ways to proceed in the attempt to fulfill one's aspiration within the jurisdiction of sastra. The first is to follow some particular process, such as karma, without recourse to bhakti. This strategy will not be successful, because, of its own accord, karma lacks the power of Bhagavan's intrinsic potency, of which bhakti is constituted, and which alone is capable of effecting the result. The second is to follow the process in question while also taking support from bhakti. In this case, the practitioner considers bhakti as subordinate to his chosen path. This approach may, however, yield the desired result, because it draws support from the internal potency through the performer's indirect participation in bhakti. In the case of the karma-marga, bhakti awards the result of the prescribed action so that the performer does not lose faith in sastra. In this approach, however, making bhakti secondary to karma is disrespectful to bhakti. Since bhakti's pleasure is not invoked by such an attitude, she offers only that which is desired by the performer and nothing more.

The third and optimal method is to take to bhakti alone, even if one still has material desires. The person who does so does not undermine the value of bhakti, although his seeking of material boons reflects a lack of acumen. Kṛṣṇa not only awards such persons the fruit that they desire but He also gives them the experience of Himself. This engenders a sense of detachment from material pleasures, whereupon such a worshiper desists from petitioning for further material boons. This is the meaning of the statement that bhakti gives rise to vairagya as a concomitant effect (SB 1.2.7), cited in Anuccheda 4. 
In SB 5.19.27, the devas refer to Bhagavan's feet as iccha-pidhānam - "that which shuts down or places a lid on desires." If it were not so, the devotee would keep on petitioning for one boon after another; material desires are never satiated merely through indulgence. Bhakti, however, does bring an end to desire, because the bliss derived from bhakti is superior to any material pleasure. This was seen in the case of Dhruva, who worshiped Visņu with the desire to obtain a kingdom greater than that of his father. When, however, Bhagavan Visņu personally appeared before him, Dhruva became fully satisfied and no longer wished for anything else. His testimony submitted at that time to Bhagavan Viṣṇu is reported in Hari-bhakti-sudhodaya: 
Being desirous of a position of great worldly importance, I took to the performance of penance. Though I was searching for broken glass, I have attained You, a divine jewel, who are unobtainable even by the gods and great sages. O Lord! I am now completely fulfilled and ask for no other boon. (Hari-bhakti-sudhodaya 7.28)

Rather, one should engage exclusively in bhakti and for the sake of the pleasure of Bhagavan alone. At present, the majority of people do not fit in any of these four categories. They are primarily interested in earning money and enjoying their senses without following any sort of spiritual process. Śrī Jīva Gosvāmī does not even bother to speak about them.
