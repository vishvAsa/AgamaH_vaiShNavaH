## Roots
Contained within the Mahābhārata, the Nārāyaṇīya teaches that the divine reality is comprised of a processual formation (vyūha) that, taken as a whole, is Viṣṇu-Nārāyaṇa himself. Each facet of the divine person (Nārāyaṇa, Vāsudeva, Saṃkarṣaṇa, Pradyumna, and Aniruddha) corresponds to a succession of states within each human being (thinking, feeling, willing, knowing, acting) and also to the process whereby a person loses track of, and then recovers, an awareness of their true nature. 

The Vaiṣṇava Āgamas of the Pañcarātra descend from this tradition, and they preserve some of its key features, such as the story of the journey to White Island (śvetadvīpa) and the recurring incarnation down through the ages of Nara and Nārāyaṇa, for example in the form of Arjuna and Kṛṣṇa. 

### shaiva inflence
Even the earliest Pañcarātra Āgamas to which we have access, such as the Svayambhuvapāñcarātra, the Vāsudevakalpa, and the Devāmṛtapañcarātra, have some parallels in the Niśvāsa corpus, but represent an earlier form of the Pañcarātra tradition than what is found in the Jayākhyasaṃhitā, which is more dependent on Śaiva and Śākta systems. 

Some later works in the tradition, such as the Lakṣmītantra and the Ahirbudhnyasaṃhitā, have even been directly influenced by the writings of the Non-dual Śaiva exegetes of Kashmir, such as Kṣemarāja.

## 3 Jewels
The Pañcarātra Āgamas are organized according to a canon of “three jewels,” the Jayākhya, the Sātvata, and the Pauṣkara. Each work is focused on a different pantheon. 

For example, while Narasiṃha appears only once in the Jayākhya, the man-lion plays a pivotal role in the Sātvata, especially in the chapters on initiation. 

## Śrīvaiṣṇavism
As both a source of ritual and theology, the Pañcarātra plays a foundational role for the tradition we call Śrīvaiṣṇavism, with two of the most important ācāryas of the tradition, Yāmuna and Vedāntadeśika, writing śāstric defenses of the legitimacy of the tradition, along with a ritual manual ascribed to Rāmānuja himself. 

Much as with later South Indian Śaivism, the early modern period sees the wide circulation of a large corpus of Pañcarātra scriptures focused on the construction and management of temples and the conducting of festivals, many of which are dialogical with the Tamil tradition of the Āḻvārs.
