This story uses five narrative frames to discuss the bipartite process of birth and death in the terms of the richly complex tripartite process (triguna) of matter, which G. J. God weaves both cosmos and person out of them and follows the same pattern for both, but turns the person inside out, as we shall discuss in the introduction to part III. This tripartite process also reveals unexpected complexity in other dyads, such as good and bad, knowledge and ignorance, and the righteous and the wicked.

From it will arise greed (lobha), leading to desire (kama) and anger (krodha), and they may lead him to employ mantra and ritual for goals opposed to his young delight in Krishna.2 The Man-lion story vividly illustrates the problem and its solution.
