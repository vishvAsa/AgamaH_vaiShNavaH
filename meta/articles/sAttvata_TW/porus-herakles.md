Porus was not a Buddhist.  
Although we cannot be absolutely sure of his personal beliefs, he was most probably a follower of Bhagavatism.

## Porus and Hercules
With regard to the army of Porus, Quintus Curtius Rufus mentions:

> Hercūlis simulācrum agminī peditum praeferēbātur. Id maximum erat bellantibus incitāmentum, et dēseruisse gestantēs mīlitāre flagitium habēbātur. Capitis etiam sanxerant poenam hīs, quī ex aciē nōn rettulissent, metū, quem ex illō hoste quondam concēperant, etiam in religiōnem venerātiōnemque conversō.  
> 
> "An image of Herculēs was carried before the ranks of the infantry: this was the greatest incentive to the combatants, and to have deserted those carrying it was considered a military disgrace. They had even sanctioned the death penalty for those who failed to bring it back from the battlefield, as the fear they had once conceived for that enemy had been converted even into religious awe and veneration."

The quote of Quintus Curtius Rufus states that the men in the infantry carried a simulacrum of Heracles, implying some sort of fixed iconography around the time of Alexander (4th century BC).

The excerpt of Quintus Curtius Rufus suggests that this "Heracles" was originally a divinized warrior/hero, which suits Vāsudeva and Saṅkarṣaṇa rather than Yakṣas (tree spirits) or Rudra (venerated as a deity since Vedic times).

### bhAgavata proximity
Not only was cultic veneration of Vāsudeva and Arjuna known to Pāṇini who lived in Śalātura in the 5th century BC (vāsudevārjunābhyām vun, with anuvṛtti so'sya bhaktiḥ), but it was also sufficiently popular in early 2nd century BC Takṣaśilā for Agathocles to mint coins in their image.

Additionally, some form of Bhagavatism had even reached the inner Himalayas by the 1st century AD, as evidenced by the Chilas II petroglyph from Gilgit Baltistan dating to the early 1st century which depicts figures with plow and discus alongside a Kharoṣṭhi label "Rama-[kri]ṣa."

Porus' kingdom sat between the Jhelum and Chenab rivers, making it less distant from the Bhāgavata "heartland" (Śūrasena kingdom) than the aforementioned locales of Śalātura (modern NWFP), Takṣaśilā (Pothohar Plateau), and Chilas II (Karakorum Highway in Gilgit Baltistan).

## Herakles
### Iconography
Vedic deities (w/ the exception of Rudra) were also depicted aniconically or with highly ambiguous features.

On the other hand, the iconography of the Vṛṣṇi brothers Kṛṣṇa and Balarāma is well attested around the 3rd-2nd century BC, only around a century after the time of Alexander.

The only other Indian deities with anthropomorphic iconography around the same time are Yakṣas (e.g. Parkham Maṇibhadra) and Rudra-Śiva (Gudimallam Liṅga).

The early 2nd century BC silver drachms of the Indo-Greek King Agathocles of Bactria (190-180 BCE), discovered at Ai Khanoum are considered by historians & numismatists to be the earliest securely datable, unambiguous anthropomorphic depictions of Indian deities.

Although Agathocles ruled over Bactria and the coins were discovered in Ai Khanoum archaeological site in Afghanistan, the coins were minted in Takṣaśilā.

These silver drachms depict Saṅkarṣaṇa-Balarāma (identifiable by the plough and pestle) on the obverse and Vāsudeva-Kṛṣṇa (identifiable by the wheel/discus and conch shell) on the reverse.

A similar depiction of Saṅkarṣaṇa and Vāsudeva (as well as their sister Ekānaṃśā) from Tikla in Madhya Pradesh dates to around the same time period (3rd-2nd century BC) and another depiction of the triad dating to the early 1st century AD has recently been discovered at Berenike, Egypt, a port city frequented by Indian sailors.

A Śuṅga-era (~2nd century BC) figure of Vāsudeva/Viṣṇu bearing a conch & wheel/discus from Malhār, Chattīsgaṛh is the earliest free-standing votive figure of an Indian deity aside from the aforementioned Śivaliṅga at Gudimallam temple.

### vAsudeva or sankarShaNa
With regard to this Indian Hercules, Megasthenes (quoted in Arrian of Nicomedia) states:

> "τοῦτον τὸν Ἡρακλέα μάλιστα πρὸς Σουρασηνῶν γεραίρεσθαι, Ἰνδικοῦ ἔθνεος, ἵνα δύο πόλιες μεγάλαι, Μέθορα τε καὶ Κλεισόβορα· καὶ ποταμὸς Ἰωβάρης πλωτὸς διαρρέει τὴν χώρην αὐτῶν."
>
> "This Hērakles [is said] to be especially honored by the Sourasenoi (Śūrasenas), an Indian people, among whom there are two great cities, Méthora (Mathurā) and Kleisóbora, and a navigable river called the Iōbáres (Yamunā) flows through their land."

Megasthenes' claim that Heracles was particularly adored by the Sourasenoi of Méthora supports the identification with Vāsudeva-Kṛṣṇa or Saṅkarṣaṇa-Balarāma, as Śūra or Śūrasena is listed as an ancestor of Vāsudeva-Kṛṣṇa, hence the epithet Śauri. Additionally, Brahmanical (e.g. Mahābhārata, Harivaṃśa), Jaina (Uttarajjhayaṇa-Sutta), and Buddhist (Ghaṭa-paṇḍita Jātaka) sources are unanimous in associating figures like Vāsudeva-Kṛṣṇa, Saṅkarṣaṇa-Balarāma, Akrūra, Ugrasena, Kaṃsa, et al. with Mathurā and the Śūrasenas.


### Not Indra 
The "Heracles" of Porus' army is most likely either Vāsudeva-Kṛṣṇa or Saṅkarṣaṇa-Balarāma interpreted via "Interpretatio Graeca," not Skanda/Kārttikeya or Indra.

The depictions of Indra/Śakra and Brahman in the Bharhut and Sanchi Stupas are invariably depicted with ambiguous features & as subservient to Buddha rather than as independent objects of veneration.

The earliest non-ambiguous depictions of Indra come from Bhaja Caves in Mahārāṣṭra (~1st Century BC) and Kuṣāṇa era Mathurā (~2nd century AD, depicted with Trimurti features such as kirīṭa-mukuṭa [Viṣṇu], third eye [Śiva], and kamaṇḍalu [Brahman]).

### Not skanda
The earliest depictions of Skanda/Kārttikeya comes Yaudheya coinage around the 1st Century BC.



### Not buddha
Depictions of Buddha were initially aniconic, e.g. an empty throne, the feet of the Buddha marked with the wheel of Dharma, vedikā around the Bodhi tree representing Buddha's enlightenment, riderless horse representing Buddha's mounting of the royal white stallion Kanthaka during his abhiniṣkramaṇa, etc.
The first anthropomorphic depiction of Buddha dates to the 1st century AD, nearly five centuries after the time of Alexander.
