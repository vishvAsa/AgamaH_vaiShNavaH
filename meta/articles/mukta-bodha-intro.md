The early modern period (c. 1400–1800) sees the emergence of at least two canons of Tantric literature that became deeply influential among the Vaiṣṇava bhakti traditions of North and North-East India. One such stream of revelation venerates Kṛṣṇa and his dalliances with the gopīs of Vṛndāvana, which are recast in the language of tantric ritual and visualization. These texts became extremely important for the followers of Caitanya of the Gauḍīya saṃpradāya and are cited repeatedly, for example, in the Haribhaktivilāsa of Caitanya’s disciple Sanātana Gosvāmī.

The other, even less well-studied tradition, exemplified in Muktabodha’s collection by the Agastyasaṃhitā, is dedicated to imagining Rāma and Sītā as the divine couple whose exploits are to be ritually imitated. This elaborate system of worship demands an entirely new system of mantras and liturgy, resulting in the production of a substantial unpublished paddhati literature, such as the Rāmārcanacandrikā of Ānandavana. Though foundational to the tradition of the Rāmarasikas, Rāma-centric tantras have never been the focus of sustained academic inquiry, and it is hoped that the MIRI Digital Library will make possible a deeper understanding of this neglected religious community.
